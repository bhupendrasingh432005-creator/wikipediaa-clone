World War II
Article Talk Edit History

World War II or the Second World War, often abbreviated as WWII or WW2, was a global war that lasted from 1939 to 1945. It involved the vast majority of the world's countries—including all of the great powers—forming two opposing military alliances: the Allies and the Axis powers. In a total war directly involving more than 100 million personnel from more than 30 countries, the major participants threw their entire economic, industrial, and scientific capabilities behind the war effort, blurring the distinction between civilian and military resources.

World War II was the deadliest conflict in human history, resulting in 70 to 85 million fatalities, with more civilian deaths than military deaths. Tens of millions of people died due to genocides, premeditated death from starvation, massacres, and disease.

Chronology

The start of the war in Europe is generally held to be September 1, 1939, beginning with the German invasion of Poland; Britain and France declared war on Germany two days later. The dates for the beginning of war in the Pacific include the start of the Second Sino-Japanese War on July 7, 1937, or the earlier Japanese invasion of Manchuria, on September 19, 1931.

Date Event
September 1, 1939 Germany invades Poland
September 3, 1939 Britain and France declare war on Germany
June 22, 1941 Germany invades the Soviet Union (Operation Barbarossa)
December 7, 1941 Japan attacks Pearl Harbor
June 6, 1944 D-Day: Allied invasion of Normandy
May 8, 1945 Victory in Europe Day (V-E Day)
August 6-9, 1945 Atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki
September 2, 1945 Japan surrenders; V-J Day

Participants

The war pitted the Allies against the Axis powers. The Allies were led by the United Kingdom, the Soviet Union, the United States, and China (the "Big Four"). Other major Allied nations included France, Canada, Australia, and many others.

Major Allied Powers

  • United States (from December 1941)
  • Soviet Union (from June 1941)
  • United Kingdom
  • China
  • France

Major Axis Powers

  • Nazi Germany
  • Empire of Japan
  • Kingdom of Italy (until 1943)

European Theater

Outbreak of War

On September 1, 1939, Germany invaded Poland under the false pretext of a Polish attack. Two days later, France and the United Kingdom declared war on Germany. Poland fell within weeks, divided between Germany and the Soviet Union under their Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact.

In 1940, Germany launched a series of successful campaigns:

  • April-June: Invasion of Denmark and Norway
  • May-June: Invasion of France, Belgium, Netherlands, and Luxembourg
  • July-October: Battle of Britain (air campaign)

Eastern Front

On June 22, 1941, Germany launched Operation Barbarossa, the invasion of the Soviet Union. This opened up the Eastern Front, which became the largest and bloodiest theater of war in human history. Key battles included:

  • Battle of Moscow (1941-1942) - First major German defeat
  • Siege of Leningrad (1941-1944) - 872-day siege resulting in mass starvation
  • Battle of Stalingrad (1942-1943) - Turning point on the Eastern Front
  • Battle of Kursk (1943) - Largest tank battle in history

Pacific Theater

Japan sought to dominate Asia and the Pacific. On December 7, 1941, Japan launched a surprise attack on the US naval base at Pearl Harbor, bringing the United States into the war.

Major battles and campaigns in the Pacific included:

  • Battle of Midway (June 1942) - Decisive US victory and turning point
  • Guadalcanal Campaign (1942-1943) - First major Allied offensive
  • Island-hopping campaign - US strategy to capture key islands
  • Battle of Iwo Jima (February-March 1945)
  • Battle of Okinawa (April-June 1945)

Home Fronts and Production

The war effort required massive industrial mobilization. The United States became known as the "Arsenal of Democracy," producing vast quantities of weapons, vehicles, ships, and aircraft.

End of the War

Germany surrendered unconditionally on May 8, 1945 (Victory in Europe Day). Adolf Hitler had committed suicide on April 30. The war in Europe was over, but fighting continued in the Pacific.

On August 6 and 9, 1945, the United States dropped atomic bombs on the Japanese cities of Hiroshima and Nagasaki. On August 15, Japan announced its surrender, which was formally signed on September 2, 1945 aboard the USS Missouri in Tokyo Bay.

Aftermath

The aftermath of World War II saw the emergence of the United States and Soviet Union as rival superpowers, setting the stage for the Cold War. Europe was divided by the Iron Curtain. The war also led to:

  • Formation of the United Nations (1945)
  • Nuremberg Trials and Tokyo Trials for war crimes
  • Decolonization of Africa and Asia
  • Marshall Plan for European recovery
  • Division of Germany and later Berlin
  • Establishment of Israel (1948)

Casualties and War Crimes

World War II was the deadliest military conflict in history. Total deaths are estimated at 70-85 million, approximately 3% of the 1940 world population.

China 3,000,000-4,000,000 15,000,000-20,000,000 18,000,000-24,000,000
Germany 4,300,000-5,530,000 1,200,000-3,000,000 5,500,000-8,530,000
Poland 240,000 5,620,000 5,860,000
Japan 2,120,000 550,000-800,000 2,670,000-2,920,000

The war was marked by significant war crimes and crimes against humanity, including:

  • The Holocaust - Systematic genocide of six million Jews and millions of others
  • Atomic bombings - Hiroshima and Nagasaki
  • Strategic bombing - Targeting of civilian populations
  • Prisoner of war massacres
  • Forced labor and death marches
